My journey.

Not a résumé — a record of how I came to build things, how I think while building them, and where all of it is pointed.

01 Origins

How I started building.

What drew me into building wasn't just writing code — it was the entire journey from identifying a problem to seeing a real solution in the hands of users.

I became fascinated by the process of understanding a problem, generating ideas, designing solutions, building products, deploying them, and learning from how people actually use them. The most rewarding part isn't shipping a feature; it's discovering whether it genuinely fills a gap, solves a real problem, and creates meaningful impact.

That way of thinking pushed me to learn technologies far beyond the classroom. Every project became an opportunity to understand systems more deeply, improve my engineering skills, and develop better products.

Today, I enjoy building at the intersection of software engineering, AI, and product thinking — where ideas evolve into products, and products evolve through the people who use them.

02 Method

How I think.

Before writing a single line of code, I spend time understanding the problem deeply.

I start with a pen and paper — mapping out user flows, system architecture, feature dependencies, and the boundaries of what should and should not be built. I prefer clarity before implementation.

Good software is usually the result of good thinking rather than fast coding.

I visualise the structure of the product long before it exists in code: how users will interact with it, how information moves through the system, and where complexity might emerge in the future.

While building, I constantly review, iterate, and refine. Features rarely reach their best form in the first version, so I improve them repeatedly until they feel simple, intuitive, and robust.

When something breaks or I get stuck, I see it as part of the process rather than a setback. Every bug fixed, bottleneck removed, and edge case discovered strengthens both the product and my understanding of the system.

03 Motive

What drives me.

I am drawn to problems that sit at the intersection of technology and human behaviour.

AI interests me because it changes what software can fundamentally do — software no longer has to just follow instructions; it can understand, adapt, reason, and assist in ways that were impossible before.

Startups interest me because they force clarity. There are limited resources, limited time, and no room for building things that nobody needs. The challenge is not writing more code, but understanding the real problem well enough to build the right thing.

Research interests me for a similar reason. I enjoy going beyond using existing tools — understanding why systems work, where they fail, and what remains unsolved.

What keeps me building is a simple question:

"Does this solve a real problem, and does it create meaningful value for the people using it?"

If the answer is yes, I can spend months thinking about it, building it, and improving it.

04 Horizon

Long-term vision.

My long-term goal is to understand intelligence at its foundations.

I want to study cognition and neuroscience deeply, explore how the brain learns and reasons, and eventually contribute to mathematical models of intelligence that can lead to more advanced AI systems.

Beyond AI, I am equally fascinated by the universe itself — observing patterns in nature, understanding physical laws, and exploring the principles that govern reality.

For me, technology and science are both ways of answering the same question: how does intelligence emerge from the universe around us?
