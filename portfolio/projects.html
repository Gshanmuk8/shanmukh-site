Collected works · Ch. II — The plates

The plates.

Five projects, each one starting from a real problem instead of a technology. For each, I've written the problem, my approach, and what building it taught me — with source code and live demos throughout.

Plate I.

FitAI

AI-Powered Fitness Coach & Progress Tracker

Stack — JavaScript · React · Vite · Node.js · Express.js · Supabase · Google Gemini API

the plates follow in order of making
  • Spotted a common problem: people struggle with accountability and tracking their progress on their fitness journey.
  • Designed and built a full-stack AI fitness platform where users can log workouts, track progress, and get personalised coaching.
  • Added secure login, real-time data handling, and AI insights using the Google Gemini API.
  • Built a multi-provider AI cascade with health checks and automatic fallback — if Gemini goes down, it falls back to OpenRouter, and then to Groq — so the AI responses stay reliable even within free-tier limits.
  • Took the whole thing from the initial idea and problem discovery all the way to deployment and real use, and learned how products change based on user feedback.
  • Focused on features that actually help with consistency, motivation, and accountability instead of just tracking workout data.
  • Kept iterating on the platform based on testing and how people used it, to figure out what actually gives users value.
  • Got real experience balancing engineering decisions, user experience, and product priorities while shipping an AI app.
Field note

The hardest part wasn't the AI — it was reliability. A coach that sometimes doesn't respond is worse than no coach at all, which is the whole reason the provider cascade exists.

Plate II.

SkillMesh

Community-Driven Skill-Sharing Platform

Stack — TypeScript · React · Vite · Node.js · Express.js · Supabase

  • Saw an opportunity for a platform built around people actually swapping skills with each other instead of just passively sharing content.
  • Designed and built a prototype where users can find others by skill, show what they can teach, and connect to learn.
  • Used product thinking to validate the idea through fast prototyping and iterating on features.
  • Looked into ways to match users based on complementary skills, interests, and learning goals so they'd actually make useful connections.
  • Focused on making it easier to find mentors, collaborators, and peers in both local and online communities.
  • Designed the platform around actually participating and sharing knowledge, not just passively consuming content.
  • Used quick iteration and feature experiments to figure out which interactions actually gave users value.
  • Learned a lot about community-driven products, user engagement, and how hard it is to build network-based platforms.
Field note

Network products live or die on the first interactions. Most of my iteration went into that one moment where two people with matching skills actually find each other.

Plate III.

LocalLens

Zero-Friction Local Discovery Platform

Stack — React · Vite · OpenStreetMap · Leaflet.js · Nominatim API · Overpass API

  • Noticed how annoying regular map apps are — you have to search manually, go through a bunch of screens, and deal with accounts before you can even find places near you.
  • Built a privacy-first local discovery app that instantly shows nearby restaurants, cafés, hotels, parks, attractions, and shops using real-time GPS.
  • Cut out a lot of the manual effort of normal map apps — no repeated searches, no switching categories, no typing in your location — you just open the app and immediately see what's around you.
  • Added automatic location updates and radius-based filtering so the recommendations stay relevant as you move around.
  • Built the whole thing on open map data using OpenStreetMap, Nominatim, and Overpass APIs — a fully serverless setup that costs nothing to run.
  • Kept it simple and fast by dropping accounts, tracking, social features, and extra UI clutter, to make a real "open and discover" experience.
Field note

This one was on purpose all about taking things away: no accounts, no tracking, no feed. The whole product is basically the three seconds between opening the app and seeing what's around you.

Plate IV.

San-Loc

Tourist Safety System — Smart India Hackathon

Stack — HTML · CSS · JavaScript · Node.js · Express.js · MongoDB · JWT Authentication

  • Designed a tourist safety solution using digital ID verification, geofencing, and rule-based emergency alerts for high-risk areas.
  • Looked into how location data and geofencing could spot high-risk areas ahead of time and trigger safety recommendations based on the situation.
  • Built rule-based emergency workflows that can alert authorities or emergency contacts in critical situations.
  • Focused on cutting down response time in emergencies with automated alerts and location-aware help.
  • Thought through the tricky balance between user privacy, security, and accessibility when designing safety-critical systems.
  • Got good experience turning a real public safety problem into a scalable tech solution under hackathon time limits.
Field note

Safety-critical systems flip the usual trade-offs: a false alarm is annoying, but missing a real one is unacceptable. Designing around that imbalance — on a hackathon clock — was the real lesson for me.

Plate V.

MarketMind

AI-Powered Competitive Intelligence Platform

Stack — TypeScript · Next.js · React · Tailwind CSS · Supabase · Prisma · Inngest · OpenRouter

  • Saw a few early startups tracking their competitors by hand — spreadsheets, bookmarked pricing pages, checking rival sites week after week — so I built MarketMind so they can look at their competitive landscape whenever they want.
  • Designed it to be a briefing, not a dashboard: you put in one URL, and in about 60 seconds it reads the product the way an analyst would.
  • Built automatic competitor discovery that maps out and ranks the whole competitive landscape from that one URL.
  • Set up continuous monitoring of thousands of public signals — read around the clock, so the market gets watched even when the team isn't looking.
  • Made sure raw events never hit the user directly: every signal comes already thought through, with why it matters and a suggested action attached.
  • Kept facts, inferences, and recommendations clearly separate — verified sources are labelled as verified, and AI inferences come with their own confidence scores.
Field note

Most tools just hand you a dashboard and leave the thinking to you. MarketMind is meant to be a briefing — the first thing you open every morning, so you know your market before it moves.
